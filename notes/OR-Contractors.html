URGENT: New Oregon Law Creates Massive Liability for Property Owners

Senate Bill 426 - Effective January 1, 2026 - Could Cost Property Owners Thousands in Unpaid Contractor Wages

Executive Summary

Effective Date

January 1, 2026 - Less than 12 months away

Liability Type

Joint and several liability - You pay even if contractor was paid in full

No Escape Clause

Cannot be waived, transferred, or indemnified by contract

Potential Costs Include:

  • • Unpaid wages + penalties (up to 30 days wages per worker)
  • • Attorney fees and court costs
  • • Interest and damages
  • • Fringe benefit contributions

Exemptions:

  • • Owner's principal residence
  • • Projects with 5 or fewer units
  • • Union workers with wage recovery mechanisms

Financial Impact Analysis

Immediate Costs

Up to 30 days wages per affected worker, plus attorney fees, interest, and damages

Property Value Impact

Increased liability exposure reduces property attractiveness to buyers and lenders

Insurance Gaps

Most standard policies don't cover wage theft liability - new coverage needed

Objection Handling Guide

Objection: "I always pay my contractors on time"

Response: "That's great, but under SB 426, you're liable even when you pay contractors in full. If your contractor doesn't pay their subcontractors, those workers can sue you directly. It's not about your payment practices - it's about the payment practices of everyone in the construction chain."

Follow-up: "The law specifically states that liability exists even when the owner paid the contractor the full amount owed."

Objection: "I'll just put it in my contracts"

Response: "Unfortunately, that won't work. SB 426 explicitly states that any agreement to waive or indemnify owners for this liability is invalid. You cannot contract your way out of this responsibility."

Follow-up: "This is one of the few laws where traditional risk transfer mechanisms don't work."

Objection: "I don't do much construction"

Response: "The law applies to all construction, reconstruction, alteration, maintenance, moving, or demolition. Even routine maintenance like HVAC repair, plumbing, or painting could trigger liability if subcontractors aren't paid."

Follow-up: "Any property improvement project could potentially expose you to this liability."

Objection: "I'm not interested in selling"

Response: "I understand, and you don't have to sell. But you should know what this law means for your property value and future marketability. Many owners are getting updated valuations to understand their exposure and what their options are."

Follow-up: "Even if you don't sell now, understanding your current position gives you better options for the future."
