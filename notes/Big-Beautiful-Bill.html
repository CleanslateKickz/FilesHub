One Big Beautiful Bill Act: Impact on Commercial Real Estate

Signed into law on July 4, 2025, by President Trump, this act introduces key tax provisions affecting commercial real estate (CRE) owners, particularly in retail. Explore the provisions, pros, cons, and broader impacts below.

Provision Details Effective Date Impact on Retail CRE
Sec. 199A Pass-Through Deduction Permanently set at 20% for pass-through entities (e.g., LLCs, partnerships). Upon enactment (July 4, 2025) Reduces taxable income for retail property owners, enhancing cash flow for reinvestment.
Carried Interest No changes, maintaining current tax treatment. N/A Neutral impact; missed opportunity for reform to align with long-term retail investments.
Sec. 1031 Like-Kind Exchanges Preserved, allowing tax-deferred property exchanges. N/A Enables retail property owners to swap properties without immediate tax liability, supporting portfolio growth.
Sec. 899 (Retaliatory Tax) Removed, eliminating barriers to foreign investment in CRE. Upon enactment Encourages foreign capital inflow, potentially increasing demand for retail properties.
Business SALT No limitations on property tax deductibility. N/A Lowers operating costs for retail properties by allowing full deduction of property taxes.
Individual SALT Cap increased to $40,000 for taxpayers earning less than $500,000, reverting to $10,000 after 5 years. Upon enactment; reverts in 2030 Temporary relief for personal tax burdens, but reversion may complicate long-term planning.
100% Bonus Depreciation & 163(j) EBITDA Made permanent and retroactive, allowing immediate deduction of capital investments and interest limitations. Upon enactment; retroactive to prior years Supports retail property improvements (e.g., store renovations) by reducing tax liability.
REIT Assets Asset limit for Taxable REIT Subsidiaries increased from 20% to 25%. Taxable years beginning after Dec 31, 2025 Enhances flexibility for retail-focused REITs, potentially increasing investment in retail properties.
Estate and Gift Tax Exemption Permanently raised to $15M, indexed for inflation. Upon enactment Simplifies wealth transfer for retail property owners, preserving family-owned assets.
Opportunity Zones Second round of designations starting July 1, 2026, with new reporting requirements and gain deferral adjustments. Amendments generally after Dec 31, 2026 Encourages retail development in underserved areas, potentially boosting foot traffic and property values.
  • Sec. 199A Pass-Through Deduction: Permanently set at 20%, benefiting pass-through entities like LLCs and partnerships, common in retail property ownership.
  • Carried Interest: No changes, maintaining current tax treatment for investment managers.
  • Sec. 1031 Like-Kind Exchanges: Preserved, allowing tax-deferred property exchanges, ideal for retail portfolio expansion.
  • Sec. 899 (Retaliatory Tax): Removed, eliminating barriers to foreign investment in CRE.
  • Business SALT: No limitations on property tax deductibility, reducing operating costs for retail properties.
  • Individual SALT: Cap increased to $40,000 (reverts to $10,000 after 5 years), affecting personal tax planning.
  • Bonus Depreciation & 163(j) EBITDA: 100% bonus depreciation and interest limitations made permanent and retroactive, aiding retail property improvements.
  • REIT Assets: Increased to 25% for Taxable REIT Subsidiaries, enhancing flexibility for retail-focused REITs.
  • Estate and Gift Tax Exemption: Permanently raised to $15M, indexed for inflation, simplifying wealth transfer for retail property owners.
  • Opportunity Zones: Second round of designations with program adjustments, encouraging retail development in underserved areas.
  • Significant Tax Savings: The permanent 20% pass-through deduction reduces taxable income for retail property owners using LLCs or partnerships. The 100% bonus depreciation allows immediate deductions for capital investments, such as store renovations or tenant improvements.
  • Investment Flexibility: Preserved 1031 exchanges enable tax-deferred swaps of retail properties, encouraging portfolio growth and diversification without immediate tax liability.
  • Attracting Foreign Capital: Removal of Sec. 899 eliminates barriers to foreign investment, potentially increasing demand for retail properties.
  • Lower Operating Costs: No limits on business SALT deductions allow full deductibility of property taxes, reducing costs for retail property owners.
  • Estate Planning Benefits: The $15M estate tax exemption, indexed for inflation, simplifies wealth transfer for retail property owners, preserving family-owned retail assets.
  • Opportunity Zones for Retail: New Opportunity Zone designations expand tax-advantaged investment opportunities, potentially boosting retail development in underserved areas with increased foot traffic.
  • Temporary SALT Cap Increase: The individual SALT cap increase to $40,000 reverts to $10,000 after 5 years, potentially increasing personal tax burdens for retail property owners with significant personal income.
  • Opportunity Zone Complexity: Adjustments to the Opportunity Zone program may introduce new compliance requirements, increasing administrative costs for retail developments.
  • No Carried Interest Reform: The lack of changes to carried interest taxation misses an opportunity to align tax treatment with long-term retail CRE investments.
  • Retroactive Provision Challenges: Retroactive application of bonus depreciation may require amending prior tax filings, creating administrative burdens for retail property owners.
  • Government: Permanent tax deductions and increased estate tax exemptions may reduce federal revenue, potentially limiting public spending on infrastructure or services that benefit retail areas.
  • Economy: Preserving 1031 exchanges and Opportunity Zones is likely to stimulate retail CRE investment, boosting local economies through job creation and increased consumer activity in retail centers.
  • Taxes: Retroactive bonus depreciation simplifies tax planning but may strain IRS resources, potentially delaying processing for retail property owners filing amended returns.
  • Foreign Investment: Removal of Sec. 899 is expected to attract global capital, strengthening the retail CRE market by increasing demand for properties.
